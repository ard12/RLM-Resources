Central Argument of This Survey Recursion in neural NLP has undergone three distinct paradigm shifts: from structural composition (tree-structured RvNNs, 2010–2015), through recurrent memory (SSMs, block-wise Transformers, 2018–2023), to inference-time self-invocation (RLMs, 2025–present). Understanding why each paradigm displaced the last is at least as important as cataloguing what each achieved.

This bibliography started as an attempt to reconcile two research traditions that share a name but not a mechanism. "Recursive neural networks" in the early 2010s meant parse-tree-guided composition — Socher et al. building phrase representations bottom-up from syntax trees. "Recursive language models" in 2025 means something structurally different: LLMs that call themselves programmatically at inference time to process inputs far beyond their context window. The name is preserved; the mechanism is almost unrecognizable.

That tension turned out to be the interesting question. Why did tree-structured RvNNs — which achieved genuine compositional insight — largely disappear after 2018 when Transformers scaled more easily? And why, having abandoned explicit recursion for flat attention, are researchers now reintroducing recursion at a different level of abstraction? The documents in this guide are selected with those questions in mind, not just as a complete inventory.

Papers are linked directly to arXiv, ACL Anthology, or project pages where available. A small number of links may drift over time; the arXiv identifiers are stable and should serve as the canonical references. Coverage skews toward the 2025–2026 RLM wave since that literature is moving fast and is harder to track. The classical RvNN literature from Stanford NLP is well-documented and easier to locate independently.

A note on scope: this guide does not attempt to cover all recurrent models, all memory-augmented Transformers, or the full SSM literature. It follows the recursion thread specifically. Mamba and S4 are included because they represent the most competitive alternative architectural paradigm, not because they are recursive in any strict sense.

Terminology: "RvNN" = tree-structured Recursive Neural Network (Socher lineage). "RLM" = Recursive Language Model in the 2025 sense (Zhang, Kraska & Khattab). These are not the same thing. The survey argues they are part of a single conceptual lineage, but that argument needs to be made — it is not obvious.

§ 01

Foundational Definitions & Early Recursive Architectures

The Stanford NLP lineage, roughly 2010–2015. These papers establish recursive composition as a principled approach to meaning: vectors propagate up parse trees, combining at each node under a learned composition function. The results were genuinely impressive for their time. What this framing required, though, was a reliable external parser — and that dependency turned out to be a significant practical liability once BERT arrived and made parsing optional.
Foundational Architecture Landmark — Read in Full

Recursive Deep Learning for Natural Language Processing and Computer Vision (PhD Thesis)

Richard Socher · Stanford University · 2014

If only one document from the classical RvNN era is read in full, this should be it. The thesis synthesizes everything Socher developed from 2010–2013: composition functions, parse-tree-guided learning, sentiment analysis, relation classification, visual-linguistic grounding. It is also unusually well-written for a dissertation — the motivation sections are worth reading as intellectual history, not just technical reference.

PDF (Stanford)
Foundational

Learning Continuous Phrase Representations and Syntactic Parsing with Recursive Neural Networks

Socher, Manning & Ng · NIPS Workshop 2010

The paper that began the modern RvNN tradition. It is worth reading alongside the thesis rather than instead of it — the thesis situates it more clearly within the broader program. The core idea (bottom-up vector composition over syntax trees) is here in embryonic form.

PDF (Stanford NLP)
Foundational Benchmark Landmark

Recursive Deep Models for Semantic Compositionality Over a Sentiment Treebank

Socher et al. · EMNLP 2013

This paper did two things that turned out to matter independently. The Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) it introduced became a standard benchmark well beyond RvNN research — it is still cited in 2025 papers that have nothing to do with recursive architectures. The Recursive Neural Tensor Network (RNTN) itself was quickly surpassed, but the dataset outlasted the model by a decade. Read the benchmark contribution separately from the architectural one.

Note: most post-2018 papers use SST as a dataset while ignoring the RNTN architecture entirely. That split is itself revealing about what in this work had lasting value.

ACL Anthology PDF
Foundational Architecture

Semantic Compositionality Through Recursive Matrix-Vector Spaces (MV-RNN)

Socher, Huval, Manning & Ng · EMNLP 2012

Extends RvNNs with matrix-vector representations — every node stores both a vector (the meaning) and a matrix (how it modifies its neighbors). The motivation was to model negation and scope, which additive composition systematically fails at. The MV-RNN showed early promise on this problem, though it remained difficult to scale. This paper is worth revisiting when reviewing how recursive architectures handle semantic operators, since the problem it targets — compositional modification — has not been fully solved by Transformers either.

ACL Anthology
Foundational Architecture Landmark

Improved Semantic Representations From Tree-Structured LSTM Networks

Tai, Socher & Manning · ACL 2015

Tree-LSTMs are the most direct architectural successor to RvNNs: the LSTM gating mechanism is adapted to operate over arbitrary tree structures rather than linear sequences. The paper demonstrated substantial improvements over chain LSTMs on sentiment classification and semantic relatedness, and for a brief period it felt like the natural direction for the field. In retrospect, it arrived just as BERT-style pretraining was beginning to make syntactic scaffolding unnecessary. Read it as the peak of the classical paradigm — not as a beginning.

ACL Anthology arXiv
Foundational

Semi-Supervised Recursive Autoencoders for Predicting Sentiment Distributions

Socher, Pennington, Huang, Ng & Manning · EMNLP 2011

Introduces reconstruction error as a training signal for recursive phrase representations. Architecturally simpler than the RNTN, but the semi-supervised framing was ahead of its time. Less essential than the 2013 and 2015 papers but useful for completeness.

ACL Anthology
The Displacement Question By 2018–2019, RvNNs had largely disappeared from mainstream NLP. BERT and its successors offered stronger performance without requiring parse trees, and the dependency on external syntactic structure that had been a feature became a liability at scale. The Tree-LSTM paper (above) arguably marks the ceiling of what classical RvNNs achieved before this displacement. The survey should address this transition explicitly: the abandonment of explicit recursion was not a judgment that recursion was conceptually wrong — it was a judgment that the particular form it took (syntax-dependent tree composition) was not the right level of abstraction for general NLP.
§ 02

Seminal RvNN Extensions: Logic, NLI & Syntax-Free Trees

The papers in this section pushed the classical paradigm into harder territory — logical semantics, natural language inference, and tree induction without external parsers. Some of this work is underappreciated precisely because it was published just as the field shifted away from RvNNs. The CRvNN paper in particular (2021) is worth reading as evidence that recursive composition was not exhausted as an idea — it was simply abandoned before its full potential was explored.
Architecture Reasoning Underappreciated

A Recursive Neural Network Model That Can Learn Logical Semantics

Bowman, Potts & Manning · EMNLP 2015

One of the more conceptually ambitious papers in the classical period. Bowman et al. showed that RNTNs can learn the meaning of logical operators from labeled tree structures — negation, conjunction, disjunction — not just approximate them. This directly anticipated questions that chain-of-thought and formal reasoning research would return to a decade later. The paper is rarely cited today outside its own immediate lineage, which is a minor bibliographic injustice.

ACL Anthology arXiv
Benchmark Landmark — Dataset Endures

A Large Annotated Corpus for Learning Natural Language Inference (SNLI)

Bowman et al. · EMNLP 2015

SNLI was explicitly motivated by the kind of compositional, parse-tree-structured reasoning that RvNNs were designed for. The irony is that it quickly became a benchmark where flat-attention models outperformed recursive ones. That result deserves analysis in the survey — it is not self-evidently a win for Transformers so much as evidence that the SNLI task did not fully operationalize the compositional demands that motivated its creation.

ACL Anthology
Architecture

Ordered-Neuron: Integrating Tree Structures Into Recurrent Neural Networks (ON-LSTM)

Shen, Tan, Sordoni, Courville · NeurIPS 2019

ON-LSTM is a rare example of a model that induces tree structure without an explicit parser and without departing from the sequential RNN framework. The ordered neuron inductive bias causes the model to spontaneously develop constituency-like representations. That it appeared in 2019 — after the Transformer wave had crested — meant it received less attention than it deserved. Still relevant for the latent tree induction thread of this survey.

arXiv
Architecture Underappreciated

Continuous Recursive Neural Networks (CRvNN)

Chowdhury & Caragea · ACL 2021

This is worth more attention than it typically receives. CRvNN extends classical tree recursion to differentiable, continuous tree induction — no discrete parsing decisions, no external syntactic supervision. The results on length generalization (particularly ListOps) are striking, and this paper makes a credible case that the compositional power of recursive architectures had not been exhausted when the field moved on. Whether CRvNN's approach scales is an open question; the paper's argument that it should is worth engaging seriously.

§ 03

Modern Recursive Language Models (2025–2026)

The 2025 wave. This is where the name "recursive" reappears in a completely different sense: not tree-structured composition of token vectors, but an LLM calling itself programmatically at inference time, managing unbounded inputs through a sandboxed execution environment. The conceptual leap from §01 to here is large enough that the survey needs to argue for continuity — it is not obvious that these belong in the same literature review at all. The argument for including them together is the Universal Transformer in §04, which provides a structural bridge.
Modern RLM Architecture Landmark — Start Here

Recursive Language Models

Alex L. Zhang, Tim Kraska, Omar Khattab · MIT / Stanford · arXiv Dec 2025

The defining paper of the new paradigm. The core idea is disarmingly simple: treat long inputs as external environment variables in a Python REPL, let the LM write Python code to decompose and recursively call itself over manageable sub-inputs, and accumulate results. The recursion here is not architectural — no weights are shared across tree nodes — but computational: the same model is invoked multiple times with structured state passing between calls. RLM-Qwen3-8B, the first model trained natively for this behavior, is included in the evaluation. The gap between RLM and RAG / summarization baselines on the OOLONG benchmark is striking and probably deserves its own section in the survey.

arXiv 2512.24601 Project Blog GitHub
Modern RLM Theory

Recursive Models for Long-Horizon Reasoning

arXiv 2603.02112 · March 2026

The formal theoretical treatment that the 2025 paper somewhat glosses over. Proves that any computable problem is modularizable into recursive sub-tasks fitting within a bounded context, and that a constant-depth Transformer can serve as the transition function of a recursive machine. The finding that recursion depth is critical — that shallow recursion degrades to simpler context management strategies — provides a testable claim the survey should engage with directly.

arXiv 2603.02112
Modern RLM Efficiency

Mixture-of-Recursions (MoR): Learning Dynamic Recursive Depth

arXiv 2507.10524 · 2025

A natural follow-on to the RLM paper: if recursion depth matters, can models learn to assign different depths to different tokens? MoR introduces routing mechanisms for this, with KV-cache sharing across recursion steps cutting memory to roughly half of a vanilla Transformer. This is an infrastructure efficiency paper more than a conceptual one, but it matters for deployment.

arXiv 2507.10524
Modern RLM Bridge

Universal Transformers

Dehghani, Gouws, Vinyals, Uszkoreit, Kaiser · ICLR 2019

The missing link between the classical and modern paradigms. Universal Transformers apply the same Transformer block repeatedly at each depth step — parameter sharing in the depth dimension — with a Pondering mechanism for dynamic halting. This can be interpreted as depth-wise recursion over representations, though it was not framed explicitly in those terms at the time. It is worth asking in the survey why Universal Transformers did not achieve widespread adoption despite strong theoretical properties: the answer probably involves training instability and the difficulty of learning good halting policies.

arXiv 1807.03819
Modern RLM Infrastructure Practical Implementation

Prime Intellect RLMEnv & INTELLECT-3 Implementation

Prime Intellect Research · Jan 2026

Industrial implementation of the RLM paradigm. Useful as a companion to the theoretical papers — it shows what actually works in practice (root LM controlling a sandboxed Python REPL, sub-LM tool calls, answer variable accumulation) and where the approach currently struggles (consistent gains on DeepDive and OOLONG, but noisier results on open-ended mathematical reasoning). The blog post is more candid about failure modes than most academic papers.

Prime Intellect Blog
Modern RLM Memory

Recurrent Memory Transformer (RMT)

Bulatov, Kuratov, Burtsev · NeurIPS 2022

Special memory tokens attached to segment-level processing, passed recurrently across segment boundaries. No architectural changes to the base Transformer. RMT is genuinely elegant in its minimalism and probably still underused relative to its conceptual cleanliness. The comparison with RLMs is instructive: RMT handles long context through implicit compressed state; RLMs handle it through explicit structured program execution. Both work; they fail differently.

arXiv 2207.06881
Modern RLM Memory

Leave No Context Behind: Infini-Attention

Munkhdalai et al. · Google · arXiv 2404.07143 · 2024

Computes both local attention within a fixed window and an incrementally updated global memory — in a single Transformer block, no segment discarded. The engineering is clean. Infini-attention is probably the strongest purely architectural long-context approach currently, though it requires modifying the base model rather than working on top of an existing one as RLMs do.

arXiv 2404.07143
Modern RLM Memory

Titans: Learning to Memorize at Test Time

Behrouz et al. · Google DeepMind · arXiv 2501.00663 · 2025

Adds neural long-term memory modules updated via gradient descent during inference — test-time learning as a memory mechanism. The results are strong and the conceptual framing is provocative: if you can update weights during inference, what is the boundary between training and test time? That question connects more deeply to the RLM paradigm than the paper makes explicit.

arXiv 2501.00663
§ 04

Efficient Attention, Block-Wise Recursion & SSMs

The practical long-context alternatives. These models matter for the survey because they represent the competing answers to the same question: how do you process more than a context window's worth of input? Mamba and S4 answer through selective state compression; Ring Attention through distributed block processing; RLMs through explicit recursive program execution. Including these without arguing for how they differ from each other — and from RLMs — would be a missed opportunity.
Architecture SSM Landmark

Mamba: Linear-Time Sequence Modeling with Selective State Spaces

Gu & Dao · COLM 2024 · arXiv 2312.00752

The most prominent Transformer alternative of the last two years. Selective state transitions — input-dependent rather than fixed — give Mamba the content-based reasoning that made SSMs previously uncompetitive with attention. Linear sequence-length scaling and 5× throughput gains over comparable Transformers are practically significant. The honest assessment: Mamba is strong up to certain context lengths and then plateaus in ways that RLMs do not, because selective compression inevitably loses information. The survey should characterize that tradeoff rather than simply listing Mamba as a competitor.

arXiv 2312.00752
Architecture SSM Infrastructure

Efficiently Modeling Long Sequences with Structured State Spaces (S4)

Gu, Goel & Ré · ICLR 2022 · arXiv 2111.00396

The foundational SSM paper underlying Mamba. The HiPPO theory for parameterizing state matrices is the technical core — it is what allows S4 to maintain stable gradients over very long sequences. Near-linear complexity and strong Long Range Arena results. Essential background for understanding why Mamba's selective extension was necessary rather than merely incremental.

arXiv 2111.00396
Architecture

RWKV: Reinventing RNNs for the Transformer Era

Peng et al. · EMNLP 2023 · arXiv 2305.13048

RWKV is notable for demonstrating that recurrent models can match Transformers at 7B+ parameter scale — a claim that was widely disbelieved until this paper. The linear recurrence with WKV attention mechanism is less theoretically elegant than Mamba but proved more tractable to scale. Include it as evidence that the recurrent paradigm was never truly exhausted, just temporarily outscaled.

arXiv 2305.13048 ACL Anthology
Architecture Infrastructure Infrastructure — Quietly Essential

FlashAttention-2: Faster Attention with Better Parallelism

Dao · ICLR 2024 · arXiv 2307.08691

Infrastructure work that quietly enabled most of the long-context experiments in this entire survey. IO-aware exact attention: tiling computation to minimize HBM memory reads/writes, achieving 2–4× speedup over FlashAttention-1. It is easy to overlook how much of the apparent progress in long-context modeling is actually hardware-level efficiency improvement rather than architectural innovation. FlashAttention-2 is the clearest example of that dynamic.

arXiv 2307.08691
Architecture

Transformer-XL: Attentive Language Models Beyond a Fixed-Length Context

Dai et al. · ACL 2019

Segment-level recurrence via cached key-value memory — the first Transformer to handle long context through an explicit recurrent mechanism. Transformer-XL established the cached-KV paradigm that RMT, Infini-attention, and others would later extend. Its 80% longer dependency capture over standard Transformers looks modest now but was genuinely significant in 2019.

ACL Anthology arXiv
Architecture Worth Revisiting

Investigating Recurrent Transformers with Dynamic Halt

Ray Chowdhury & Caragea · arXiv 2402.00976 · 2024

A systematic diagnostic study of Universal Transformer variants with dynamic halting. The finding that chunk-wise recurrent Transformers outperform dense universal transformers on state-tracking and parity tasks is more interesting than the abstract suggests. This is exactly the kind of paper that gets overlooked in favor of larger-scale results but that provides critical evidence about what recursive depth actually buys, computationally speaking.

§ 05

Existing Surveys & Reviews

The existing survey landscape is patchy. There is a reasonable survey of classical RvNN/RNN architectures (arXiv:2510.17867), a solid review of efficient attention methods, and strong domain-specific surveys on mathematical reasoning and theorem proving. What does not yet exist — as of March 2026 — is a survey that explicitly connects the classical tree-recursive tradition to modern inference-time recursive LMs. That gap is the justification for this survey.
Survey Baseline — Read First

A Survey of Recursive and Recurrent Neural Networks

arXiv 2510.17867 · Oct 2025

The most recent broad survey of the classical recursive/recurrent space and the clearest structural baseline for organizing the early sections of this literature review. The taxonomy — General, Structured, and Hybrid categories covering Grid RNNs, Hierarchical RNNs, Tree RNNs, etc. — is reasonably principled. Notable limitation: it does not cover 2025-era RLMs at all, treating the survey as a history of a completed paradigm rather than a living one. The gap between this survey's endpoint and the RLM papers is precisely where a new contribution can intervene.

arXiv 2510.17867

Efficient Attention Mechanisms for Large Language Models: A Survey

arXiv 2507.19595 · July 2025

Systematic overview of linear attention approximations, sparse patterns, block-wise routing, and compressive memory approaches. The hardware-algorithm co-design sections are particularly useful for situating why certain architectural choices are preferred in practice over theoretically stronger alternatives. Pairs well with the FlashAttention-2 and Ring Attention papers above.

Specialized Applications

Recursive and hierarchical architectures applied to specific high-value domains. Math Reasoning

NERHRT: Hierarchical Recursive Tree Decoding for Math Word Problems

ScienceDirect 2023

Proposes a hierarchical recursive tree decoder that aggregates ancestor node information for solving math word problems, with decimal-notation numerical embeddings and dual-direction graph attention. State-of-the-art on four MWP benchmarks at time of publication. A representative example of RvNN applied directly to structured mathematical generation — useful for demonstrating that classical recursive ideas were still productive in 2023 for domain-specific tasks even as they had been abandoned for general NLP.

Benchmark Long-Context

OOLONG & OOLONG-Pairs Benchmarks

Referenced in Zhang et al. 2025 RLM paper

Designed to be unsolvable by approximate attention and standard RAG. OOLONG requires linear-complexity global aggregation over all entries in a structured dataset; OOLONG-Pairs requires quadratic pairwise reasoning. These are among the few benchmarks where the failure mode of non-recursive approaches is clearly observable rather than merely statistical. The benchmark design deserves discussion in the survey as an example of how evaluation choices shape what counts as a long-context "solution."

See RLM paper (arXiv 2512.24601)
Long-Context

MemWalker: Interactive Memory for Long-Context Dialogue

Chen et al. · arXiv 2310.05029 · 2023

Constructs a tree-like data structure over input text that an LM navigates when answering long-context questions. MemWalker occupies an interesting intermediate position between static summarization and the full RLM paradigm — it uses tree structure without full self-invocation. The comparison with RLMs in the Zhang et al. paper is instructive and worth reproducing in the survey.

arXiv 2310.05029
Long-Context Survey

Beyond the Limits: Survey of Techniques to Extend Context Length in LLMs

Wang et al. · arXiv 2402.02244 · 2024

The most comprehensive landscape survey of context extension techniques: position interpolation, attention approximation, memory augmentation, context compression. Maps the full solution space within which RLMs sit as an inference-time alternative. § 07

Influential Researchers & Laboratories

These are the investigators whose work most directly defines the recursive architecture research tradition. Use them as seeds for citation network analysis on Semantic Scholar — trace who cites whom and when, rather than treating each paper as an isolated contribution.

Richard Socher

Stanford NLP (formerly) → Salesforce

The defining figure of classical RvNNs. His 2010–2014 output — the 2013 RNTN/SST paper, the 2014 thesis — established the foundational vocabulary. Also co-invented GloVe, which separates him from the purely structural thread of this survey.

Christopher Manning

Stanford NLP

Co-supervisor of Socher's RvNN program; led the Stanford Parser that provided structural input to those models. Manning's sustained interest in syntax-aware NLP spans the entire period this survey covers, making his group's output a useful longitudinal baseline.

Alex L. Zhang

MIT CSAIL

Primary author of the 2025 RLM paper and creator of RLM-Qwen3-8B. PhD student at MIT; follow his arXiv preprints closely given the speed at which this sub-field is currently moving.

Omar Khattab

Stanford / DSPy

Co-author of the RLM paper; created DSPy, which has added native RLM support. His research trajectory from retrieval-augmented generation toward compositional LM programs makes him the clearest intellectual bridge between the RAG era and the RLM era.

Albert Gu

Carnegie Mellon / Together AI

Lead inventor of S4 and Mamba. The HiPPO memory theory he developed is the most principled treatment of long-range dependency in the non-attention literature. Follow his work as the primary source for claims about what SSMs can and cannot represent.

Tri Dao

Stanford / Together AI

Co-inventor of Mamba and FlashAttention. FlashAttention (1 & 2) is the infrastructure that makes all modern long-context experiments tractable — Dao's systems perspective on ML hardware is often underrepresented in purely algorithmic surveys.

Mostafa Dehghani

Google DeepMind

First author of Universal Transformers — the conceptual bridge between classical depth-wise recursion and modern RLMs. Also led Vision Transformer work, showing the breadth of his architectural thinking.

Samuel Bowman

NYU / Anthropic

Created SNLI and demonstrated that RvNNs can learn logical semantics. His transition to large-scale RLHF work at Anthropic is itself a data point about the trajectory of the field — from structured compositional models toward learned human preference alignment.

Tsendsuren Munkhdalai

Google

Author of NTI (syntax-free tree indexers), Infini-attention, and Titans (test-time memory). The consistent thread across all three is hierarchical or persistent memory — arguably the most sustained single-researcher contribution to recursive memory in NLP.

Yikang Shen

MIT / IBM Research

Author of ON-LSTM and subsequent syntax-aware language models. Key figure for the latent tree induction thread — the attempt to recover structural computation from sequential models without explicit syntactic supervision.

§ 09

Proposed Taxonomy for Chapter Organization

A working taxonomy — not a definitive one. The categories below are designed around the survey's central argument (three paradigm shifts) rather than purely by technical type. Some papers belong in multiple categories; where that happens, the tension is usually worth noting rather than resolved by an arbitrary assignment.
Paradigm I — Structural Composition
  • Tree-guided composition (RvNN, RNTN)
  • Gated recursive cells (Tree-LSTM)
  • Matrix-vector modification (MV-RNN)
  • Logical semantics in trees (Bowman 2015)
  • Continuous / parser-free recursion (CRvNN)
Dominant paradigm 2010–2018. Displaced by BERT-scale pretraining, not shown to be theoretically inferior.
Paradigm II — Recurrent Memory
  • Segment caching (Transformer-XL)
  • Memory tokens (RMT)
  • Selective state spaces (S4, Mamba, RWKV)
  • Compressive attention (Infini-attention)
  • Test-time gradient memory (Titans)
Dominant paradigm 2018–2024. Efficient but lossy — compression is unavoidable.
Paradigm III — Inference-Time Recursion
  • Programmatic self-invocation (RLM 2025)
  • Dynamic recursion depth (MoR)
  • Theoretical grounding (arXiv:2603.02112)
  • Industrial implementation (Prime Intellect)
Emerging paradigm 2025–present. No architectural modification required; exact information preservation; requires tool use capability.
Bridging Architectures
  • Universal Transformers (depth-wise sharing)
  • ON-LSTM (latent tree induction)
  • Recurrent Transformers w/ dynamic halt
  • MemWalker (tree navigation)
These resist clean paradigm assignment. That resistance is analytically useful — they show continuity where the paradigm narrative implies rupture.
Performance Benchmarks
  • SST (sentiment — classical era)
  • SNLI / MultiNLI (NLI)
  • Long Range Arena (LRA)
  • OOLONG / OOLONG-Pairs (RLM-specific)
  • BrowseComp-Plus (multi-hop QA)
  • LoCoDiff (code history tracking)
  • GSM8K / MATH (mathematical reasoning)
Application Domains
  • Sentiment analysis (SST, RNTN)
  • Natural language inference
  • Math word problems / equation generation
  • Formal theorem proving (Lean, Isabelle)
  • Code generation / tracking
  • Long-document QA
§ 10

Open Questions & Research Frontiers

A literature survey that only catalogues the past is less useful than one that positions its reader to generate new work. The questions below are not rhetorical — they are genuinely unresolved, and some of them point toward specific experimental designs or theoretical gaps.

Is recursion a computational primitive or an engineering workaround?

The 2026 theoretical paper (arXiv:2603.02112) argues that recursion depth enables computation that constant-depth Transformers cannot perform. If true, this is a strong claim that should be empirically testable. If false — if RLMs are simply chunking with bookkeeping — the paradigm needs a more modest framing.

Can recursive depth be learned end-to-end without collapse?

Universal Transformers with dynamic halting largely failed to achieve widespread adoption. MoR attempts learned routing for recursion depth. Neither has demonstrated stable, consistent convergence across diverse tasks. This may be the central training dynamics challenge for recursive architectures.

What is the relationship between classical RvNN compositionality and modern RLM recursion?

The survey's central claim — that these belong in the same intellectual lineage — is asserted in the thesis banner but not yet proven. What would it mean to operationalize this connection? Is there a formal reduction between tree-structured composition and inference-time self-invocation?

Does recursive inference generalize to algorithmic reasoning without explicit tool scaffolding?

Current RLMs require Python REPL access and tool-use capability. Whether the recursive strategy can be induced via prompting alone — without a sandboxed executor — is unclear. This question matters for deployment contexts where tool access is unavailable.

Why did RvNNs lose to Transformers, and is that reason still valid?

The conventional answer is scale: BERT-style pretraining worked better, so parse-tree supervision became unnecessary overhead. But CRvNN's 2021 length generalization results suggest compositional recursion may have strengths that weren't properly evaluated before abandonment. The question is whether those strengths matter at scales current hardware allows.

How should RLMs be evaluated against Mamba and SSMs on tasks requiring exact information preservation?

OOLONG was designed to expose SSM and RAG failures specifically. A benchmark designed to expose RLM failures would be a genuine contribution. What tasks require the kind of globally coherent compression that SSMs do well but recursive chunking cannot provide?
